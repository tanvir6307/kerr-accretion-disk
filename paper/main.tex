\title{When does thermal continuum fitting misread black-hole spin?\\
A validated synthetic experiment with marginalized, multi-epoch fits}

\author{Tanvir Hassan}
\affiliation{Independent researcher}
\email{tanvir6307@gmail.com}

\date{\today}

\begin{abstract}
Thermal continuum fitting infers black-hole spin by tying the observed disk
spectrum to the radius of its inner edge. The inference relies on assumptions
about spectral hardening and the stress at the innermost stable circular orbit
(ISCO) that are known to be uncertain. We build a validated, reproducible
Kerr thin-disk simulator---registered geodesic and Page--Thorne physics, a
correctness-first adaptive ray tracer, image-plane transfer maps, and physically
normalized diluted-blackbody spectra---and use it to run a controlled
model-misspecification experiment. We generate detector-independent synthetic
spectra for a fiducial \SI{10}{\Msun} black hole at \SI{8}{\kilo\parsec} and fit them with
a joint, color-marginalized posterior sampled by an affine-invariant ensemble
sampler over a spectral emulator. Across 48 locked conditions and 4800 noise
replicates we find three results. First, marginalizing the color correction
removes most of the spin bias that a fixed-color fit incurs: color-only
misspecification recovers spin nearly unbiased (mean $|\Delta a_\ast|\approx
0.01$) with good fit quality. Second, neglecting a weak nonzero ISCO stress
($\Delta\eta=0.02$) biases the recovered spin strongly (mean $|\Delta
a_\ast|\approx 0.48$, up to $\approx 1.2$), largest for retrograde and low spin
and negligible near-extremal; crucially, the fit quality degrades detectably
($\chi^2/\mathrm{dof}\approx 9$). Third, jointly fitting two luminosity states
narrows the spin interval in 21 of 24 groups (mean width reduction $\approx
32\%$) but leaves the systematic bias essentially unchanged. These are
sensitivity-experiment results for an idealized detector-free thin disk, not
observational spin errors; the simulator is not a GRMHD calculation. All
quantitative statements are reproducible from versioned configurations and are
linked to evidence files in a machine-readable claim audit.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

The temperature and normalization of an optically thick, geometrically thin
accretion disk encode the radius of its inner emitting edge, and hence---under
the assumption that the disk terminates at the ISCO---the dimensionless Kerr spin
$a_\ast = Jc/(GM^2)$. This is the basis of the thermal continuum-fitting method
for stellar-mass black holes~\cite{McClintockNarayanSteiner2014}. The inference
chain runs through relativistic disk structure, spectral hardening in the disk
atmosphere, and radiative transfer through the curved spacetime, and it is
sensitive to two systematic uncertainties in particular: the color-correction
(spectral-hardening) factor $f_{\rm col}$ that relates the color temperature to
the effective temperature~\cite{DavisBlaesHubenyTurner2005,DavisHubeny2006,SalvesenMiller2021},
and the possibility of a nonzero magnetic stress at the ISCO that alters the
inner disk structure~\cite{AgolKrolik2000,KulkarniPennaShafee2011,PennaMcKinneyNarayan2010}.
Recent work continues to emphasize that departures from the idealized
Novikov--Thorne disk affect spin inference~\cite{MummeryIngramDavisFabian2024,MummeryEtAl2025RapidOrPlunging}.

The same ingredients make an excellent setting for a controlled
uncertainty-quantification experiment: synthetic spectra generated under one set
of assumptions can be fitted under another to isolate exactly when an apparently
precise spin estimate is biased. This paper does not analyze observational data.
It asks a narrower, falsifiable computational question:

\begin{quote}
In a validated, detector-independent Kerr thin-disk synthetic campaign, how does
controlled spectral-hardening and weak inner-stress misspecification affect the
recovered spin when the color correction is \emph{marginalized}, and does joint
multi-epoch fitting reduce the resulting interval width or bias?
\end{quote}

Our contribution is fourfold: (i) a verified, open Kerr thin-disk spectral
simulator with an adaptive ray tracer and physical absolute normalization; (ii)
an independent analytic cross-validation of the tracer; (iii) a joint,
color-marginalized inference layer with simulation-based calibration on the
actual forward model; and (iv) quantitative bias and coverage maps from a
pre-declared misspecification design. The answer is derived only from versioned
repository outputs and the claim audit in \texttt{paper/claim\_audit.md}.

\section{Physical model}

All production formulae are registered with their source and convention in
\texttt{docs/equation\_registry.md}. Internally the code uses geometric units
$G=M=c=1$, Boyer--Lindquist coordinates $(t,r,\theta,\phi)$, and a signed
dimensionless spin $a_\ast$, with positive $a_\ast$ denoting a prograde disk. The
Kerr metric, horizon, equatorial circular-orbit energy, angular momentum and
angular velocity, and the analytic ISCO radius follow
Bardeen, Press \& Teukolsky~\cite{BardeenPressTeukolsky1972}.

The baseline disk is geometrically thin, optically thick, equatorial, and
time-steady. The zero-torque surface flux follows the Page--Thorne
conservation-law form~\cite{PageThorne1974},
\begin{equation}
F(r)=\frac{\dot M}{4\pi\sqrt{-g}_{\rm disk}}\,
\frac{-\Omega_{,r}}{(E-\Omega L)^2}
\int_{r_{\rm in}}^{r}(E-\Omega L)\,L_{,r'}\,dr',
\end{equation}
with $r_{\rm in}=r_{\rm ISCO}$, the Page--Thorne disk-area convention
$\sqrt{-g}_{\rm disk}=r$, and $E$, $L$, $\Omega$ the registered circular-orbit
quantities. A controlled nonzero-stress extension adds the Agol--Krolik flux term
parameterized by an efficiency increment $\Delta\eta\ge 0$~\cite{AgolKrolik2000};
$\Delta\eta=0$ recovers the baseline exactly, and the added luminosity integrates
to $\dot M\,\Delta\eta c^2$. We restrict the controlled domain to
$0\le\Delta\eta\le 0.1$.

The local emission is a diluted blackbody,
$I_{\nu,\rm em}=f_{\rm col}^{-4}B_\nu(f_{\rm col}T_{\rm eff})$, with
$T_{\rm eff}=(F/\sigma_{\rm SB})^{1/4}$. Color correction is treated strictly as
a sensitivity parameter, not a claim of atmosphere realism. Unlike a shape-only
calculation, the spectrum is \emph{physically normalized}: the black-hole mass
sets the gravitational radius $r_g=GM/c^2$ and, with the Eddington ratio
$\ell=L/L_{\rm Edd}$ and efficiency, the accretion rate $\dot M = \ell L_{\rm
Edd}/(\eta c^2)$; the distance sets the observed flux. The observable is the
binned energy flux in \si{\erg\per\second\per\centi\metre\squared}. Because the normalization depends
on mass and on spin (through the ISCO), it carries spin information beyond the
spectral shape. For the fiducial \SI{10}{\Msun}, \SI{8}{\kilo\parsec} system the effective
temperature peaks near $0.5\,\mathrm{keV}$, in the soft X-ray band.

\section{Numerical methods}

Null rays are initialized on a local static-observer tetrad at a large observer
radius and evolved with a scalar Hamiltonian Kerr ray tracer. The geodesic force
term uses closed-form analytic derivatives of the contravariant metric (no
finite-difference step parameter), and integration uses an adaptive
Dormand--Prince RK45 scheme with a relative/absolute error tolerance and
bisection refinement of the disk-crossing event. Each successful disk ray records
its screen coordinates, solid angle, emission radius and azimuth, redshift factor
$g=\nu_{\rm obs}/\nu_{\rm em}$, physical emission angle, and diagnostics; the
observed spectrum uses the invariance of $I_\nu/\nu^3$. The image plane is a
geometry-sized full-disk screen (spanning $\pm 1.4\,r_{\rm out}$) at observer
radius $10^3\,r_g$, so it captures the disk from the ISCO to the outer edge
without clipping. A precomputed spectral-grid emulator interpolates the
ray-traced spectrum over the fitted parameters so that a multi-parameter ensemble
sampler is affordable. Figure~\ref{fig:images} shows representative full-disk
transfer-map redshift images, and Fig.~\ref{fig:spectra} the resulting
physically normalized spectra.

The full-resolution campaign (Table~\ref{tab:design}) uses a $64\times64$
full-disk screen, a $13\times9$-node emulator over $(a_\ast,f_{\rm col})$, 24
logarithmic energy bins from \SIrange{0.1}{20}{\kilo\electronvolt}, and electron-scattering limb
darkening.

\section{Validation}

Validation proceeds bottom-up with declared tolerances recorded in
\texttt{docs/validation.md} and summarized in Fig.~\ref{fig:validation}. The
metric inverse identity, ISCO benchmark table, and Novikov--Thorne efficiency
table pass to $\lesssim 10^{-10}$; null-geodesic invariants (energy, axial
angular momentum, Carter constant) are conserved to $\sim 10^{-11}$; the
Schwarzschild shadow boundary is bracketed at $\sqrt{27}\,r_g$; the Page--Thorne
flux vanishes at the ISCO, recovers the Newtonian $r^{-3}$ scaling, and matches
the expected efficiency; and same-model injection recovery and simulation-based
calibration pass on the actual forward model.

The strongest self-contained check is an independent analytic cross-validation.
For a Schwarzschild disk observed face-on, the circular-orbit redshift is
$g(r)=\sqrt{1-3/r}$ and the observed flux is a closed-form redshifted,
area-weighted sum of diluted blackbodies with no light bending. Implemented
independently of the production ray tracer, this benchmark agrees with the full
ray-traced spectrum to $\approx 4\%$ in relative-$L_1$ norm, validating the
tracer's redshift factor, solid-angle normalization, and flux integration.
Numerical convergence is set by the screen resolution: relative-$L_1$ spectrum
differences versus a $112\times112$ reference are $1.9\%$, $0.73\%$, and $0.38\%$
at $24^2$, $40^2$, and $64^2$, all below the $3\%$ noise level
(Fig.~\ref{fig:convergence}). We do not establish agreement against an external
third-party ray-tracing code; that comparison remains open.

\section{Experiment design}

The fitted parameters are the spin $a_\ast$ and a single color correction
$f_{\rm col}$, with independent bounded uniform priors frozen in
\texttt{paper/tables/priors.csv}. The fit model is zero-torque
($\Delta\eta=0$); the misspecification is introduced only in the data-generating
truth, which may carry a color offset ($f_{\rm col}^{\rm true}\neq$ fit) and/or a
weak inner stress ($\Delta\eta^{\rm true}=0.02$). For each condition, 100
deterministic Gaussian noise replicates ($3\%$ relative error) are generated and
fitted; the spin posterior is the color-marginalized marginal. The 48 conditions
are the locked list carried forward from a frozen screening protocol and span
spin $a_\ast\in\{-0.5,0,0.5,0.9\}$, inclination $\{40,70\}^\circ$, two Eddington
ratios, two true color corrections, and $\Delta\eta\in\{0,0.02\}$. The
pre-registered estimands are the spin bias $\Delta a_\ast=\hat a_\ast -
a_\ast^{\rm true}$, its root-mean-square error, the $68\%$/$95\%$ interval
coverage, the posterior width, and the goodness-of-fit statistic.

\section{Results}

\paragraph{Marginalizing the color correction largely removes color-induced
spin bias.} When the only misspecification is a color-correction offset and
$f_{\rm col}$ is marginalized, the recovered spin is nearly unbiased: the mean
absolute bias over the zero-stress conditions is $\approx 0.013$ with good fit
quality ($\chi^2/\mathrm{dof}\approx 1.35$). This is in sharp contrast to a
fixed-color one-dimensional fit, which can bias the spin by up to $\approx 1$.
Figure~\ref{fig:biaswidth} shows the condition-level structure: the low-bias,
low-$\chi^2$ points are the color-only cases.

\paragraph{Neglecting weak inner stress biases spin strongly, but detectably.}
Fitting data with a weak nonzero ISCO stress ($\Delta\eta=0.02$) using a
zero-stress model biases the spin by a mean absolute value of $\approx 0.48$,
reaching $\approx 1.2$ in the worst conditions, where the posterior rails toward
the extremal boundary. The bias is a strong, monotonic function of the true spin
(Table~\ref{tab:results}, Fig.~\ref{fig:biasmap}): the mean absolute bias falls
from $0.82$ at $a_\ast=-0.5$ to $0.43$ at $a_\ast=0$, $0.23$--$0.30$ at
$a_\ast=0.5$, and only $0.02$ at $a_\ast=0.9$. Physically, when the true ISCO is
large (retrograde/low spin) there is ample room for the missing inner-stress flux
to masquerade as a much smaller ISCO, i.e.\ a much higher spin; near-extremal
disks are already at small ISCO and cannot be biased far. Critically, this
misspecification is \emph{detectable}: the mean $\chi^2/\mathrm{dof}$ over the
stress-misspecified conditions is $\approx 9$ (reaching $\approx 33$ in one
cell), so the model is flagged as a poor fit. Coverage collapses accordingly
(Fig.~\ref{fig:coverage}); it is also below nominal even in the clean case, a
residual of the spin/color degeneracy and finite emulator and noise budget.

\paragraph{Joint multi-epoch fitting shrinks the interval but not the bias.}
Pairing two luminosity states with a shared spin and color correction and
comparing joint against separate single-epoch fits, the joint fit reduces the
mean $68\%$ spin-interval width in 21 of 24 groups, by $\approx 32\%$ on average
(Fig.~\ref{fig:multiepoch}, \texttt{table6\_multi\_epoch\_comparison.csv}). The
systematic bias, however, is essentially unchanged (mean absolute bias
$0.327\to0.325$), and the mean coverage is not improved (it decreases from
$0.148$ to $0.096$ as the interval tightens around a biased estimate). Multi-epoch
sharing thus buys precision, not accuracy, when the model is misspecified.

\section{Discussion}

These results sharpen the standard continuum-fitting caveats into quantitative,
falsifiable statements. First, the color-correction problem is largely curable by
marginalization: a free $f_{\rm col}$ inflates the spin interval (this is the
spin/color degeneracy) but removes most of the bias that a fixed color assumption
would inject. Second, and more seriously, a weak nonzero ISCO stress that is
\emph{not} in the fitting model produces a large, systematic spin bias that is
worst exactly where continuum fitting is often applied to constrain low or
retrograde spins. The redeeming feature is that the misspecification degrades the
fit statistic, so it is in principle diagnosable from the data rather than
silently biasing the answer. Third, the intuition that ``more epochs help'' must
be qualified: shared-parameter multi-epoch fitting reduces random uncertainty but
does not correct a systematic bias, and by tightening the interval it can worsen
coverage. This is consistent with the expectation that averaging more data around
a biased model concentrates, rather than corrects, the error.

Relative to prior work, our contribution is not the existence of these
systematics---color-correction and inner-stress effects are
established~\cite{DavisHubeny2006,AgolKrolik2000,KulkarniPennaShafee2011,SalvesenMiller2021}---but
a validated, reproducible map of \emph{how large} the resulting spin bias is,
\emph{where} in the spin--inclination plane it is worst, whether it is
\emph{detectable}, and whether multi-epoch fitting mitigates it, all under a
color-marginalized posterior with simulation-based calibration on the actual
forward model.

\section{Limitations}

The simulator is a thin-disk model, not a GRMHD simulation. The spectra are
detector-independent: no instrument response, background, or calibration is
folded in, so the reported biases are not observational spin errors. Returning
radiation and photon capture are diagnosed but not reprocessed into the
likelihood. The inner-stress model is the controlled Agol--Krolik prescription,
not a self-consistent magnetic disk. Multi-epoch fitting shares spin and color
correction but fixes epoch luminosities as metadata. Same-model coverage runs
below nominal even in the clean case, so the coverage numbers are
sensitivity-experiment diagnostics rather than a calibrated real-data pipeline.
Agreement against an external ray-tracing code is not established. A single
fiducial system (\SI{10}{\Msun}, \SI{8}{\kilo\parsec}) is used.

\section{Conclusions}

Using a validated, physically normalized Kerr thin-disk simulator and a
color-marginalized joint fit, we quantify how thermal continuum fitting misreads
spin under controlled misspecification. Marginalizing the color correction
removes most color-induced spin bias; neglecting a weak ISCO stress biases the
spin strongly---largest for retrograde and low spin, up to $|\Delta a_\ast|\sim
1$---but the bias is detectable through an elevated fit statistic; and
multi-epoch fitting tightens the spin interval by $\sim 30\%$ without removing the
systematic bias. All results are reproducible from versioned configurations.
